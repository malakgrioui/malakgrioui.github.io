GearBank Initiative

A women-led sustainable livelihood initiative supporting coastal communities in North Sumatra — combining cooperative development, alternative income creation, and monitoring systems to reduce pressure on mangrove forests while building real economic opportunity.

Organisation

Global Mangrove Trust is a mission-driven non-profit driving large-scale environmental restoration through technology and community partnerships. GearBank is one of its flagship livelihood initiatives, developed in North Sumatra, Indonesia, to address the economic pressures that lead coastal communities toward mangrove-destructive activities.

Context & Challenge

Coastal communities in North Sumatra depended on livelihoods that put direct pressure on mangrove ecosystems. Protecting the forests required creating credible economic alternatives — not just awareness campaigns. GearBank was designed to support women's groups in coastal villages to produce and commercialize value-added products: mangrove-based food items, seafood snacks, soap, chips, and dim sum. The challenge was to move from existing production capacity to structured market opportunity — building cooperatives with real governance, training, packaging standards, distribution channels, and data-driven monitoring of outcomes.

Key Contributions

  • Strategic Structuring Contributed to the design of the GearBank pilot phase — translating field insights into an operational model covering people, process, and product development.
  • Cooperative Design Supported the identification of women leaders within village groups and helped structure cooperative governance roles (CEO, COO, CFO profiles) for sustainable self-management.
  • Field Research & Insight Conducted village visits to understand women's entrepreneurial capacities — documenting strengths in pricing, production, and scaling, as well as gaps in packaging, financial management, and distribution.
  • Monitoring System Design Designed reporting and monitoring tools using KoboToolbox and Airtable to track cooperative activity, production volumes, sales, and business outcomes — creating a quantifiable evidence base for economic and conservation impact.
  • Business Development Support Identified needs for stronger distribution channels, structured financial management training, and packaging improvements to help groups scale beyond village-level sales.

Methodology & Tools

  • Approach Field-first — field visits and direct engagement with women leaders drove the design before documentation.
  • Field Data Collection KoboToolbox
  • Monitoring & Tracking Airtable
  • Collaboration Google Workspace
  • Diagramming Miro

Lessons Learned

  • Conservation impact is most durable when it creates real economic alternatives — environmental protection without viable livelihoods generates short-term compliance at best.
  • Women in coastal villages often demonstrate remarkable entrepreneurial capacity: strong product knowledge, pricing intuition, and community trust. The bottleneck is rarely motivation — it's access to structure, tools, and markets.
  • Monitoring systems for community projects need to generate evidence of economic impact, not just activity logs. Quantifying income, production volume, and market reach is what moves a pilot from a good story to a fundable program.
